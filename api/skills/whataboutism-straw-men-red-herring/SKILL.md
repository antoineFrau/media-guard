## What it is
**Whataboutism** is a deflection tactic where criticism is avoided by pointing to others' faults. **Straw man** involves misrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack. **Red herring** introduces irrelevant information to distract from the main issue. These techniques are often used together to manipulate discourse.

## How to recognize it
- **Whataboutism**: Look for phrases like "But what about...", "They do it too", or "The other side is worse" (FR: "Mais qu'en est-il de...", "Ils le font aussi", "L'autre côté est pire"). The intent is to deflect criticism by shifting focus to others' faults.
- **Straw man**: Identify when an argument is misrepresented or exaggerated (FR: "homme de paille"). The intent is to misrepresent the opposition to make their argument easier to attack.
- **Red herring**: Notice when irrelevant information is introduced to distract (FR: "fausse piste"). The intent is to change the subject or derail the conversation.
- **Appeals**: These techniques often evoke emotions of deflection, counter-accusation, or distraction. The author's intent is typically to deflect criticism, misrepresent the opposition, or change the subject.

## Examples
- "President Trump —who himself avoided national military service in the 1960's— keeps beating the war drums over North Korea." (Whataboutism)
- "But what about the opposition? They take money from foreign donors too." (Whataboutism)
- "So you want to defund the police? That means you want chaos in our streets." (Straw man)
- "But look at what their side did! The other party is far worse." (Red herring)
