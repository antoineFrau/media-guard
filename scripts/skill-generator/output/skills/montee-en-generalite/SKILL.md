## What it is
Montée en généralité is a manipulation technique where a specific case or isolated event is generalized to imply a broader pattern or trend. This technique is often used to create a sense of widespread threat or to justify extreme actions or policies. It can be employed in political discourse, media narratives, or everyday conversations to sway public opinion or reinforce certain biases.

## How to recognize it
1. **Specific to General**: Look for statements that take a specific instance and generalize it to a broader context. For example, "This one incident proves that all members of this group are dangerous."
2. **Lack of Context**: The generalization often lacks context or evidence to support the broader claim.
3. **Emotional Appeal**: The technique often relies on emotional appeal rather than logical reasoning to convince the audience.
4. **Extreme Language**: The language used is often extreme, using words like "always," "never," "all," or "none" to generalize the specific case.

While specific research papers on "montée en généralité" may not be readily available, this technique is closely related to the concept of "hasty generalization" in logical fallacies and cognitive biases. Hasty generalization is a well-documented phenomenon in cognitive psychology and rhetoric, where a broad conclusion is drawn from a small sample or a single piece of evidence.

Key papers and resources include:
- Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux. This book discusses various cognitive biases, including the tendency to generalize from limited information.
- Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases. *Science*, 185(4157), 1124-1131. This seminal paper explores how people make judgments and decisions based on heuristics that can lead to biases, including hasty generalizations.
